# Intro

The Surrender Meditation Technique, thoroughly explored by Dr. David Hawkins and supported by numerous psychological and neuroscientific findings, addresses emotional management and psychological resilience through a simple yet powerful practice: consciously allowing emotions to surface, remain, and dissipate without interference or resistance.

Unlike conventional meditation techniques aimed primarily at quieting the mind or focusing attention, surrender meditation specifically targets the habitual psychological responses—such as judgment, resistance, or suppression—that sustain negative emotional states. Research suggests that allowing emotions to fully manifest without resistance significantly reduces their intensity and duration, thus promoting lasting emotional regulation and psychological health.

This site is structured to guide you systematically from theory to practical integration:

**The Book:**

Begin by exploring foundational knowledge, featuring insights from Dr. Hawkins' book" Letting Go" and excerpts clearly detailing the mechanics and benefits of surrender meditation.

**Principles:**

Delve deeper into the core psychological, evolutionary, and spiritual principles underlying the technique. Each principle is presented as a concise reflection or contemplation chunk to reinforce understanding.

**Surrender Practice:**

Implement the technique practically through a structured "Skill Tree," designed to incrementally develop your skills from controlled exercises performed alone ("Core-Practice"), to consciously chosen challenges ("Provocation"), and ultimately, everyday situations ("Interaction"). This progressive approach ensures clarity, applicability, and sustainable habit formation.

By navigating through these sections, you will acquire not only theoretical understanding but also tangible skills that translate into everyday emotional resilience, reduced stress, and greater psychological clarity. The clarity and rigor of this approach distinguish surrender meditation from broader meditation or personal development methods, making it particularly effective for scientifically oriented practitioners seeking measurable emotional and cognitive improvements.

Whether you're seeking relief from stress and negative emotions, deeper emotional resilience, spiritual growth, or a pathway to genuine inner fulfillment, this technique will guide you step-by-step—from understanding foundational principles to practical application in everyday life.

# Quick Start Guide

Ready to dive in immediately? Here’s a simple overview of the Surrender Meditation Technique to get you started right away:

1. **Recognize the Feeling:**
    
    Pause and become fully aware of the emotion or feeling present in the moment.
    
2. **Allow the Feeling Fully:**
    
    Let the feeling arise naturally, without suppressing, judging, resisting, or wishing it were different. Simply acknowledge its presence.
    
3. **Release Resistance:**
    
    Let go of all mental efforts to control or modify the feeling. Drop any judgment or moralization about having the feeling.
    
4. **Stay With the Feeling:**
    
    Allow yourself to experience the feeling fully, observing it calmly and curiously. Let it run its course naturally until it dissipates.
    

By practicing these simple steps regularly, you'll quickly notice feelings becoming less overwhelming, diminishing in intensity, and ultimately dissolving, leaving behind clarity, peace, and a deeper sense of emotional freedom.

### Hawkins Surrender Technique in a nutshell:

“Letting go involves being aware of a feeling, letting it come up, staying with it, and letting it run its course without wanting to make it different or do anything about it.”

### Hawkins Surrender Technique Excerpt from Letting Go:

Here’s an excerpt from Letting Go that explains the technique and valuable background info:

“Letting go involves being aware of a feeling, letting it come up, staying with it, and letting it run its course without wanting to make it different or do anything about it. It means simply to let the feeling be there and to focus on letting out the energy behind it.”

The first step is to allow yourself to have the feeling without resisting it, venting it, fearing it, condemning it, or moralizing about it. It means to drop judgment and to see that it is just a feeling. The technique is to be with the feeling and surrender all efforts to modify it in any way. Let go of wanting to resist the feeling. It is resistance that keeps the feeling going. When you give up resisting or trying to modify the feeling, it will shift to the next feeling and be accompanied by a lighter sensation. A feeling that is not resisted will disappear as the energy behind it dissipates.

As you begin the process, you will notice that you have fear and guilt over having feelings; there will be resistance to feelings in general. To let feelings come up, it is easier to let go of the reaction to having the feelings in the first place. A fear of fear itself is a prime example of this. Let go of the fear or guilt that you have about the feeling first, and then get into the feeling itself.

When letting go, ignore all thoughts. Focus on the feeling itself, not on the thoughts. Thoughts are endless and self-reinforcing, and they only breed more thoughts. Thoughts are merely rationalizations of the mind to try and explain the presence of the feeling. The real reason for the feeling is the accumulated pressure behind the feeling that is forcing it to come up in the moment. The thoughts or external events are only an excuse made up by the mind.

As we become more familiar with letting go, it will be noticed that all negative feelings are associated with our basic fear related to survival and that all feelings are merely survival programs that the mind believes are necessary. The letting go technique undoes the programs progressively. Through that process, the underlying motive behind the feelings becomes more and more apparent.

To be surrendered means to have no strong emotion about a thing: “It’s okay if it happens, and it’s okay if it doesn’t.” When we are free, there is a letting go of attachments. We can enjoy a thing, but we don’t need it for our happiness. There is progressive diminishing of dependence on anything or anyone outside of ourselves. These principles are in accord with the basic teaching of the Buddha to avoid attachment to worldly phenomena, as well as the basic teaching of Jesus Christ to “be in the world but not of it.”

Sometimes we surrender a feeling and we notice that it returns or continues. This is because there is more of it yet to be surrendered. We have stuffed these feelings all of our lives and there can be a lot of energy pushed down that needs to come up and be acknowledged. When surrender occurs, there is an immediate lighter, happier feeling, almost like a “high.”

By continuously letting go, it is possible to stay in that state of freedom. Feelings come and go, and eventually you realize that you are not your feelings, but that the real “you” is merely witnessing them. You stop identifying with them. The “you” that is observing and is aware of what is happening always stays the same. As you become more and more aware of the changeless witness within, you begin to identify with that level of consciousness. You become progressively primarily the witness rather than the experiencer of phenomena. You get closer and closer to the real Self and begin to see that you had been duped by feelings all along. You thought that you were the victim of your feelings. Now you see that they are not the truth about yourself; they are merely created by the ego, that collector of programs which the mind has mistakenly believed are necessary for survival.”

— David Hawkins

# Surrender Practice

## The Surrender Skill Tree Systematic

The following skill tree is a step-by-step guidance into the practice of surrender. It also serves as a possible solution to some of the more common problems that might appear when first encountering the technique.

It is often perceived as vague, to universal &/ to theoretical and unfeasible to either get a proper understanding for its concrete application or having a clear pathway to forming valuable habits.

To mitigate these challenges three different groups of application are defined with different levels of complexity, forming a practice skill tree that evolves from a controlled and isolated application into dynamic daily situations, who might involve self chosen external stimuli as also in later stages coping with known triggers:

- **Core-Practice**: This groups exercises are done in a quiet, undisturbed environment where one takes to oneself. It is for internal exploration of the surrender technique and starts of by learning to relax the body. The preceding levels are first dealing with the thought and later with the feeling dimension.
- **Challenge:** The exercises in this group involve intentionally encountering external stimuli that are challenging or uncomfortable while explicitly being under control of the practitioner. They are designed to test and strengthen one's ability to apply the surrender technique while consciously choosing the triggers and their intensity. The starting point here is to learn how to maintain composure and equanimity in the face of physical or sensory provocations, like cold showers or physical exertion. Later stages are more dynamic, multidimensional &/ lengthy, like in e.g. sport related training sessions.
- **Integration**: The final group invites to carry the technique into everyday situations. The examples chosen are a practical approach to train the application of surrender within the complexities of the individuals life. Starting of by performing beneficial day-to-day tasks that can be done in solitude such as cleaning or cooking in a continuous surrender state, the exercises evolve into e.g. grocery shopping and other tasks that involve interaction with the environment. Lastly exercises are leading to the awareness of surrendering to any minor emotion through out the day as also challenging situations or people.

Each group represents a clear, accessible way to practice and become familiar with the surrender technique, moving from theory to practical application.

## **The Skill Tree Exercises in Detail**

- **Surrender: The Raw Process by Hawkins**
    1. Recognize the feeling
    2. Do not resist the feeling
        1. Resistance is judging (moralizing, condemning), modifying or alternating (wishing for it to be different)
        2. Resistance is thinking: Thoughts are self-reinforcing rationalizations of the mind to try and explain the presence of the feeling.
    3. Be with the feeling
- **Surrender: The Raw Process by Alex**
    1. Recognize the feeling
    2. Accept the feeling, as a natural phenomenon
        1. Pitfalls:
            1. Fear or guilt: Let go of the fear or guilt that you have about feeling the or any kind feeling in the first place (e.g. shame, jealousy, fear, pride)
            2. Resistance: Judging (moralizing, condemning), modifying or alternating (wishing for it to be different), defensiveness or avoidance, labeling or mental boxing
            3. Thoughts: They are self-reinforcing rationalizations of the mind to try and explain the presence of the feeling. It’s a spiraling dynamic keeping you engaged and the feeling alive
    3. Go into the feeling:
        1. Wholehearted Welcoming: Being interested, allowing the feeling to fully be, even craving for intensity (Brewer:” Bring it on”).
        2. Safe Space: Creating a designated, visualized space for the feeling to be, maybe even to connect and interact
        3. Loving Curiosity: Where is the feeling located, does it change over time? Where does surrending the feeling lead to? Are there other feelings coming up or connected to it, do they have a common basis?

- **Nighttime Surrender Movie**
    1. **Routine**: Each night before falling asleep, mentally review the events of your day in reverse order, starting from the moment you got into bed.
    2. **Recollect:** Remember each event in reverse chronological order, moving back in time until you reach the first event of the day (e.g., waking up).
    3. **Surrender**: Focus your attention on each event briefly, acknowledging, releasing and thus surrendering it as you move backward through the day.
- **Black Roll**
    1. **Use the Black Roll**: Utilize the foam roller to apply pressure to different muscle groups.
    2. **Focus on Sensation**: As you experience discomfort or pain, concentrate on the sensation and surrender to it instead of resisting.
    3. **Observe and Release**: Pay attention to how your body reacts to the pressure and notice the relief and release as you let go of tension.
- **Cold Shower**
    1. **Begin Gradually**: Start with a regular shower, then slowly turn the water to a colder temperature.
    2. **Breathe and Surrender**: As the cold hits, breathe deeply and steadily, focusing on surrendering to the sensation rather than reacting to the shock.
    3. **Observe the Effects**: Pay attention to your body's response and thoughts that arise, practicing letting go of the urge to resist the cold.
- **Sport or Challenging Training**
    1. **Engage Actively**: Participate in a sport or physical training that challenges you.
    2. **Focus on Surrendering**: During the activity, practice surrendering to the physical demands and discomfort.
    3. **Embrace Physical Exertion**: Use this as an opportunity to let go of mental resistance and accept physical exertion as a path to self-awareness and growth.
- **Kink (If interested in sexual kink practices)**
    1. **Consent and Boundaries**: Engage in kink with a clear understanding of consent and boundaries.
    2. **Explore Surrender**: Use the experience to explore surrender in a safe and controlled environment.
    3. **Focus on Sensations and Emotions**: Pay attention to the sensations and emotions that arise, allowing yourself to experience them fully without judgment.
- **Integration: Cleaning**
    1. **Focused Attention**: While cleaning, concentrate solely on the task at hand.
    2. **Surrender to the Act**: Let go of other thoughts and distractions, surrendering to the act of cleaning.
    3. **Observe Inner Calm**: Notice how this focused state leads to a sense of inner calm and surrender.
- **Cooking**
    1. **Mindful Cooking**: Approach cooking as a meditative practice.
    2. **Be Present in Each Step**: Focus on each step of the cooking process, fully present in the moment.
    3. **Surrender to the Experience**: Let yourself be absorbed in the task, surrendering to the cooking process.
- **Taking Walks (Alone)**
    1. **Connect with Self and Surroundings**: Use the walk to connect with yourself and your environment.
    2. **Mindful Walking**: Pay attention to your breath, steps, and the environment around you.
    3. **Embrace the Moment**: Surrender to the moment, letting go of other concerns or thoughts.
- **Groceries: Surrendering to the Environment**
    1. **Be Present**: Practice being fully present while grocery shopping.
    2. **Observe Reactions**: Pay attention to your reactions to the environment.
    3. **Practice Surrendering**: Let these stimuli pass through your awareness without attachment.
- **Shopping**
    1. **Approach with Detachment**: Shop with an attitude of detachment, focusing on the experience rather than the outcome.
    2. **Mindful Choices**: Be mindful of your choices and reactions during shopping.
    3. **Let Go of Desires**: Surrender to the experience, letting go of desires and focusing on the present moment.
- **Every Moment Surrender**
    1. **Utilize Every Moment**: Use every moment and activity as an opportunity to practice surrender.
    2. **Be Present in Interactions**: Whether in conversation, work, or leisure, focus on being present and letting go of attachment to outcomes.
    3. **Observe the Effects**: Notice how this practice affects your interactions and sense of peace.
- **Emotional Situations with Babies and People with Special Needs**
    1. **Empathetic Presence**: In interactions, focus on being fully present and empathetic.
    2. **Practice Unconditional Acceptance**: Surrender to the experience, letting go of any preconceived notions or judgments.
    3. **Use as Learning Opportunities**: Use these interactions to practice patience, understanding, and unconditional acceptance.
- **Toxic Personalities**
    1. **Maintain Inner Peace**: Practice maintaining composure and inner peace when dealing with toxic personalities.
    2. **Surrender Control**: Acknowledge that you cannot control others, but can control your reactions.
    3. **Set Healthy Boundaries**: Focus on setting healthy boundaries and practicing self-care in these interactions.
