# NARM-to-Personal Observation Match Map

## Page purpose

Compare NARM theory with documented personal observations using graded confidence and explicit evidence boundaries, then identify safe, high-leverage questions and development targets without assigning a diagnosis or survival style.

## Executive summary

The strongest correspondence domains are contact/trust and regulation/protective activation. Attunement/recognition and autonomy are useful medium-confidence hypotheses. Shame and any named NARM survival adaptation remain low-confidence; love/sexuality has no usable personal evidence. The map therefore supports present-focused inquiry and small experiments, not personal classification.

## Macro — Why it matters

Why it matters: the operator wants to know where NARM genuinely illuminates repeated experience and where it merely sounds similar. Graded matching prevents conceptual enthusiasm from becoming false certainty. The result focuses effort on repeated, observable, lower-risk domains while routing developmental-history and classification questions to qualified professional exploration.

## Meso — What it is

What the match means: high correspondence requires repeated personal observations plus a clear NARM category, but still is not clinical certainty. Contact/trust and regulation meet that threshold as exploration domains. Attunement/recognition and autonomy recur but need more behavioral examples and counterexamples. Shame and a specific survival adaptation lack independent assessment and developmental evidence. Love/sexuality is not assessable from this corpus.

## Micro — How it works

How to act on it: track trigger, protection, need, and resource; distinguish present boundary information, older threat expectation, and physical depletion; regulate before consequential contact; then test trust, recognition, and autonomy through small, consent-based, reversible steps. Record disconfirming evidence. Use therapy for developmental interpretation, repeated overwhelm, or any attempt to assign a survival structure.

## Direct answers

### match-q01: Which NARM concepts have high, medium, or low correspondence with the documented personal observations?

High: contact/trust disruption corresponds with repeated reports of exclusion, betrayal, relational vigilance, difficulty trusting, and desire for honest connection; regulation/protective activation corresponds with repeated anger, vigilance, analysis, defensive presentation, and explicit regulation goals. Medium: attunement/recognition corresponds with pressure to be understood, chosen, or recognized, while autonomy corresponds with self-protection and boundary concerns; both need clearer frequency and exceptions. Low: shame and any named NARM survival adaptation lack independent assessment and developmental detail. No rating: love/sexuality has no relevant personal evidence. Next questions are whether a cue signals present danger or expected disconnection, what precedes escalation, what exact recognition is wanted, and when autonomy increases rather than reduces contact.

### match-q02: Which core needs, contact patterns, or survival adaptations are most useful hypotheses to explore with a therapist or through careful reflection?

First explore trust and contact: direct reports describe betrayal, exclusion, vigilance, and desire for connection; a behavioral inference is that perceived threat may narrow contact; the practitioner question is how to distinguish present cues, boundaries, and older expectation safely. Second explore anger, analysis, and defensive presentation as possible protection: vulnerable affect may sometimes sit beneath activation, but anger may also be proportionate current boundary information. Third explore autonomy and recognition: ask what forms of request, dependence, receiving, and self-protection preserve dignity and connection. A named survival adaptation, attachment label, or developmental cause remains unresolved and requires appropriate professional context.

### match-q03: What are the highest-leverage personal development targets suggested by the NARM-to-observation map?

1. Trigger–protection–need tracking: high fit, low energy cost, high likely impact. 2. Regulation before consequential contact: high fit, low-to-moderate cost, high likely impact. 3. Small trust/contact experiments with consent and exit options: medium-high fit, moderate cost, potentially high impact, preferably with trusted or therapeutic support. 4. Direct recognition and autonomy requests: medium fit and moderate impact. 5. Focused grief/vulnerability exploration: medium fit but higher emotional cost, requiring careful pacing and support. These are experiments, not promises of cure or proof of NARM theory.

## Source boundaries

- NARM sources define theory; personal sources provide observations. Neither alone establishes a personal developmental history or survival style.
- High correspondence means a useful exploration domain, not diagnosis or clinical confidence.
- Present relational facts and physical depletion may explain responses without a trauma-based explanation.
- Spiritual and Jungian overlap is not evidence of equivalence with NARM.

## Evolution and versions

- The match map deliberately progresses from broad repeated domains to narrower hypotheses and stops before classification; later observations should update confidence in either direction.

## Contradictions

- Anger may protect vulnerable affect or convey valid present boundary information; the function cannot be selected in advance.
- Conceptual overlap among NARM, Jung, surrender, and ACIM does not establish a combined clinical model.

## Uncertainty and open questions

- There is no independent assessment, developmental history, frequency record, or longitudinal outcome data.
- Health and energy constraints are self-reported and may interact with activation.
- Which high-confidence domain predicts repeated observations and which does not?
- What counterevidence would lower each match rating?
- Which hypotheses are confirmed, refined, or rejected in qualified therapeutic exploration?
