Reviewing strategic recommendations is essentially an exercise in "intellectual hygiene." Without a structured approach, human nature defaults to the path of least resistance: confirmation bias and groupthink.

To combat this, we divide methods into two camps: **Meta Strategy** (designing the thinking process) and **Meta Detective** (investigating the cracks in that process).

---

## 1. Meta Strategy: The Architect's Toolkit

These methods focus on **how** we think and generate the strategy. They ensure the logic is sound and the perspective is wide enough before the "audit" begins.

- **Ladder of Inference:** Helps teams trace back from their conclusions to the raw data, ensuring no logic leaps were made based on unverified beliefs.

- **Six Thinking Hats:** A parallel thinking process that forces the group to switch perspectives (Emotional, Logical, Creative, etc.) collectively rather than arguing from fixed positions.

- **Disney Method:** Segregates the creative process into three distinct rooms: the **Dreamer** (limitless ideas), the **Realist** (pragmatic planning), and the **Critic** (finding the holes).

- **Reframing Matrix:** A tool to look at a problem from different stakeholder perspectives (e.g., "How would a CFO see this?" vs. "How would a Customer see this?").


## 2. Meta Detective: The Auditor's Toolkit

These are adversarial and investigative methods designed to break the strategy and find hidden biases.

- **Pre-Mortem:** You imagine the strategy has already failed spectacularly and work backward to determine why.

- **Red Teaming:** A full-scale adversarial simulation where a separate team attempts to disrupt or defeat the proposed strategy.

- **Devil’s Advocacy:** Assigning a specific person the formal role of finding every reason why the recommendation is wrong.

- **Assumption Testing:** Explicitly listing every "load-bearing" assumption and grading them by certainty and impact.

- **Confirmation Bias Checks:** Searching for evidence that explicitly _contradicts_ the recommendation.

- **Base-rate Reasoning:** Ignoring the "uniqueness" of the project and looking at how similar strategies have performed historically in the industry.


---

## 3. Strategy → Detective Handoff Questions

When the "Architects" hand the plan to the "Detectives," these questions bridge the gap:

1. **The "Kill Switch":** "What specific data point, if proven false tomorrow, would make us abandon this entire plan?"

2. **The Road Not Taken:** "What was the second-best alternative, and why did it lose by such a narrow margin?"

3. **The Filter Check:** "Which pieces of data did we intentionally exclude because they felt like 'noise'?"

4. **The Incentives:** "Who stands to gain the most—personally or professionally—if this specific recommendation is approved?"

## 4. Red-Team Checklist

Use this list to ensure your Red-Teaming exercise isn't just "polite feedback."

|**Category**|**Checklist Item**|
|---|---|
|**Adversarial**|Have we modeled a competitor’s "counter-move" that specifically targets our weakness?|
|**Logistics**|Does the strategy rely on "frictionless" execution, or have we factored in 20% delay?|
|**Culture**|Would a mid-level manager feel safe "pausing" this strategy if they saw a red flag?|
|**Blindspots**|Have we consulted a "naïve expert" (someone outside the department) for a gut check?|
|**Resources**|If our budget is cut by 15% mid-way, does the strategy survive or collapse?|

---

## 5. Common Failure Modes

- **The "Paper Tiger" Devil’s Advocate:** The role is assigned to a junior staffer who is too intimidated to actually challenge the CEO’s "pet project."

- **The Inside View:** Overestimating your own team's capabilities while underestimating external market volatility (ignoring base rates).

- **Analysis Paralysis:** Using these tools to delay decision-making indefinitely rather than to refine it.

- **False Consensus:** Mistaking silence in a meeting for agreement with the strategy.
