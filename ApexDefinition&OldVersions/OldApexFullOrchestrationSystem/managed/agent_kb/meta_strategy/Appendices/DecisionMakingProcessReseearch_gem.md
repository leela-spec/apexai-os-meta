You are essentially looking for a "Cognitive Architecture"—a structured way to move from raw data and foggy questions to high-conviction action. What you described is the holy grail of strategic consulting, military intelligence, and philosophical inquiry.

Fortunately, you don't have to reinvent the wheel. Here are five established frameworks, ranked by their **resilience** (how well they hold up under pressure), **multi-dimensionality** (how many factors they consider), and **broader appeal** (utility across different fields).

---

### 1. First Principles Thinking (The Deconstructive Approach)

Often popularized by Aristotle and more recently Elon Musk, this is the gold standard for **checking axioms**. It requires you to break a problem down to its fundamental truths—the things we know are "true" without further inference—and build a solution from the ground up.

- **Distillation:** You strip away analogies and "best practices" until you reach the bedrock axioms.
    
- **Verification:** You question every assumption (e.g., "Why does this cost $10k? Is it the material or the labor?") until you find the physical or logical limit.
    
- **Evaluation:** Logic-driven. If the fundamental pieces work, the strategy is sound.
    
- **Rank:** **#1 for Resilience.** It is the hardest to do but impossible to "break" because it doesn't rely on shaky precedents.
    

### 2. The Cynefin Framework (The Problem Verifier)

Developed by Dave Snowden, this framework is specifically designed to **verify the problem** before you even try to solve it. it categorizes problems into five domains: Clear, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic, and Confused.

- **Distillation:** It forces you to identify the _nature_ of the system you are in. Is it a "known-known" (Clear) or an "unknown-unknown" (Complex)?
    
- **Verification:** Prevents you from applying the wrong logic (e.g., using a rigid plan for a chaotic situation).
    
- **Evaluation:** Strategies are evaluated based on whether you should "Sense-Respond" or "Probe-Sense-Respond."
    
- **Rank:** **#2 for Multi-dimensionality.** It acknowledges that logic works differently depending on the environment.
    

### 3. The WRAP Process (The Decision Engine)

Created by Chip and Dan Heath, this is a highly practical 4-step model designed to neutralize the cognitive biases that usually ruin decisions (like confirmation bias or short-term emotion).

- **W**iden Your Options: Prevents "whether or not" binary choices.
    
- **R**eality-Test Your Assumptions: This is your **source and evidence verification** stage.
    
- **A**ttain Distance Before Deciding: Evaluates impact and emotional cost.
    
- **P**repare to be Wrong: Evaluates **risk and likelihood of success** via "pre-mortems."
    
- **Rank:** **#3 for Broader Appeal.** It’s the most user-friendly for both personal and business use.
    

### 4. Analysis of Alternatives (AoA)

Used heavily by the GAO and military organizations, this is a rigorous process for comparing multiple strategies against a set of baseline requirements.

- **Distillation:** It translates a vague "need" into a set of "Key Performance Parameters."
    
- **Verification:** Rigorous auditing of the evidence supporting each alternative.
    
- **Evaluation:** A heavy focus on **Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)** and **Life-cycle costs**. Each strategy is scored on a weighted matrix of risk vs. effectiveness.
    
- **Rank:** **#4 for Logic.** It is highly clinical and data-heavy, excellent for "high-stakes" resource allocation.
    

### 5. The OODA Loop (The Adaptive Process)

Developed by military strategist John Boyd, the **Observe, Orient, Decide, Act** loop is about speed and resilience in changing environments.

- **Distillation:** "Orientation" is the most important part—it’s where you filter the data through your mental models and axioms.
    
- **Verification:** Constant feedback loops. If the "Act" phase yields bad data, you immediately re-Observe.
    
- **Evaluation:** Success is measured by "tempo" and the ability to out-maneuver the problem's complexity.
    
- **Rank:** **#5 for Agility.** While it lacks the deep cost-analysis of AoA, it is the most resilient framework for fast-moving "live" problems.
